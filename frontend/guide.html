Nutrient & Deficiency Guide

Understand the signs your plants are showing and how to address them for a healthier harvest.

General Symptoms and Signs of Deficiency

  • Chlorosis: Yellowing of leaves. This is the most common symptom of nutrient deficiency and can be a sign of many issues.
  • Necrosis: Brown or black spots, or the death of plant tissue.
  • Stunted Growth: Overall small size and slow growth compared to healthy plants.
  • Discoloration: Leaves may turn a different color, such as purple, red, or bronze.
  • Wilting: Drooping leaves, even when the soil is moist.

Macronutrient Deficiencies

Macronutrients are elements that plants need in large amounts. Deficiencies in these nutrients often show symptoms on older leaves first because the plant moves these mobile nutrients to new growth where they are needed most.

1. Nitrogen (N)

Symptoms:

  • General yellowing (chlorosis) of the entire plant, starting with older leaves at the bottom.
  • Older leaves may turn a light green to yellow color, then brown and die.
  • Stunted growth and thin stems.

Causes:

  • Sandy soils, which are prone to nutrient leaching.
  • Excessive rain or overwatering.
  • Soil with low organic matter.

Solution:

  • Use a fertilizer high in nitrogen (the first number in the NPK ratio).
  • Add organic matter like compost or manure to the soil.

2. Phosphorus (P)

Symptoms:

  • Stunted growth.
  • Leaves, especially older leaves, turn a dark blue-green, reddish, or purple color, often on the underside.
  • Reduced flowering and fruit production.

Causes:

  • Very acidic or very alkaline soils.
  • Cold, wet, or waterlogged soil, as roots can't properly absorb the nutrient.

Solution:

  • Use a fertilizer with a high phosphorus content (the second number in the NPK ratio).
  • Improve soil drainage and aeration.

3. Potassium (K)

Symptoms:

  • Yellowing and browning (necrosis) of the leaf tips and margins (scorching), starting on older leaves.
  • Leaves may become crinkled or curled.
  • Weak stems and poor disease resistance.
  • Poor fruit quality and development.

Causes:

  • Sandy soils.
  • Intensive crop production, as plants can use up the available potassium quickly.

Solution:

  • Apply a fertilizer with high potassium (the third number in the NPK ratio).
  • Add compost to the soil.

4. Magnesium (Mg)

Symptoms:

  • Yellowing between the veins (interveinal chlorosis), while the veins remain green. This symptom starts on older leaves.
  • Leaves may develop reddish-brown or bronze tints.
  • Reduced plant vigor and smaller leaf size.
  • The problem is most common in light, sandy soils.

Causes:

  • Light, sandy soils prone to leaching.
  • Excessive application of high-potassium fertilizers, which can interfere with magnesium uptake.
  • Low soil pH (acidic soil).

Solution:

  • Use magnesium sulfate (Epsom salts) as a foliar spray for a quick fix.
  • Apply dolomitic lime to the soil to raise the pH and add magnesium.

Micronutrient Deficiencies

Micronutrients are elements that plants need in smaller amounts. Because these nutrients are less mobile within the plant, deficiencies often show up in new, younger leaves first.

1. Iron (Fe)

Symptoms:

  • Severe interveinal chlorosis (yellowing between the veins) on young leaves. The veins remain a distinct green color.
  • In severe cases, the entire new leaf may turn white or almost white.

Causes:

  • High soil pH (alkaline soils), which makes iron less available to the plant.
  • Poor soil aeration or overwatering.

Solution:

  • Lower the soil pH with an acidifying agent like elemental sulfur.
  • Apply an iron chelate fertilizer, which is designed to be absorbed in high-pH soils.

2. Manganese (Mn)

Symptoms:

  • Interveinal chlorosis on young leaves.
  • In severe cases, brown spots or specks may develop on the yellowed tissue.
  • Stunted growth.

Causes:

  • High soil pH (alkaline soils), which makes manganese less available.

Solution:

  • Lower the soil pH.
  • Apply a manganese foliar spray or a manganese-rich fertilizer to the soil.
